Academic Literacy Models

           

Understanding different approaches to literacy in educational contexts

                                    
                           
       
                
           
               
               
               
               
           
       
                
           
               

Study Skills Model

               
                       
  • Views literacy as individual, cognitive skills
    •                    
  • Focuses on surface language features (grammar, punctuation)
    •                    
  • Assumes skills transfer easily between contexts
    •                    
  • Based on behaviorist, transmission models of learning
    •                    
  • Often takes a "deficit" approach to student writing
    •                
           
                        
               

Academic Literacies Model

               
                       
  • Views literacy as social practices that vary by context
    •                    
  • Focuses on meaning-making, identity, power, and authority
    •                    
  • Highlights institutional nature of knowledge construction
    •                    
  • Emphasizes complexity of acquiring literacy practices
    •                    
  • Considers epistemological dimensions of writing
    •                
           
                        
               

Academic Socialization Model

               
                       
  • Focuses on acculturation into disciplinary discourses
    •                    
  • Students learn discipline-specific ways of writing and thinking
    •                    
  • Assumes disciplinary discourses are relatively stable
    •                    
  • Associated with constructivism and situated learning
    •                    
  • Once rules are learned, students can reproduce discourse
    •                
           
       
                
           

Key Differences

           

The academic literacies model differs from traditional approaches by emphasizing the social nature of reading and writing, acknowledging power relationships in literacy practices, and focusing on how writing shapes knowledge construction within academic disciplines. It views literacy as a social practice rather than just a set of technical skills to be acquired.
